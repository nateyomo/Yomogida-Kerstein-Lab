Classification

Muscle tone can be classified in different ways. When classifying tone based on the physiological process inciting the tone, we can create two classifications: Postural tone and Phasic tone1. we can also view tone based on the processes by which resistance to stretch occurs: active and Passive tone1.

Postural & Phasic tone

Postural tone is observed in axial muscles where gravity is the most important inciting factor. Gravity applies a steady stretch on the muscles and tendons, which is countered by a prolonged muscle contraction known as âpostural toneâ1.

Phasic tone is what we commonly think of as âmuscle toneâ and is found in the extremities1. Phasic tone is observed as a rapid and short duration response resulting from muscle spindle activation following a rapid stretch on the tendon and muscle1.

Physiology

Skeletal muscles do not contract without the action potential from the fibers, this tone is the product of low rate nerve impulses from the spinal cord2. These low level nerve impulses are modulated partially by the brain and partially by the muscle spindles2. The brain sends afferent signals to the anterior motoneurons of the spinal cord2. The muscle spindle within the skeletal muscle sends signals to the muscle belly itself2.

Spinal control

Stretch detection

Spinal control is dependent on sensory afferents providing information about a stretch a certain muscle and its tendon are experiencing. The intrafusal fibers of the muscle spindle detect change and rate of change in muscle length while the golgi tendon organ (GTO) are located in the tendon and detect tendon stretching1.

During a dynamic stretch, the muscle spindle detects changes in muscle length and transmits these signals via type Ia afferents1.

Tonic activity during Steady-state length is a static response which is transmitted through both type Ia and type II afferents1.

Tendon stretching detected by the GTOs is transmitted to the spinal cord via type Ib afferents1.

Stretch-reflex

These muscle spindle signals travelling through type Ia and II active the stretch-reflex1. A motor efferent signal is transmitted through alpha-motor (Î±-motor) and gamma-motor (Î³-motor) neurons1. The Î±-motor neurons activate the extrafusal fibers1. The Î³-motor neurons activate the intrafusal fibers1.

The stretch reflex can come in 2 forms: Dynamic or static1.

Dynamic stretch reflex

The dynamic type of stretch reflex is created when a muscle is suddenly and rapidly stretched1. This change in rate/velocity stimulates the annulospiral endings and the sigmal is transmitted via nuclear bag fibers and type Ia afferents to the spinal cord1.

At the spinal cord, the afferent stretch signal is received, processed, and an efferent motor signal is created1. The efferent motor signal is sent through Î±-motor efferents

Efferent motor signal from the spinal cord through Î±-motor neurons to extrafusal fibers, resulting in sudden, synchronous contraction of the muscle known as the dynamic stretch reflex1. The synchonicity of the contract makes the muscle have a strong contractile response1.

Dysfunction

Tone dysfunction can be divided into hypertonia, paratonia, or dystonia.

Spasticity and rigidity are the two types of hypertonia. These forms of hypertonia result from dysfunction in the supraspinal pathways and the interaction between spinal cord and muscle spindle1.

Dystonia and paratonia are not related to physiological dysfunction in tone pathways1. The other two disorders of altered tone, namely dystonia and paratonia, are not exactly related to the physiological dysfunction of the tone pathways1.

In the motor control system, spasticity and rigidity are primarily an âoutput systemâ problem, whereas dystonia is a âsystem level processingâ problem1.

Dystonia and paratonia have altered tone secondary to network disruption in the basal ganglia, the thalamocortical circuits, and their connections.
