Pathophysiology

The brain parenchyma lacks nociceptors, thus the headache must be referred from other sources1. Headache symptoms are caused by mechanical traction, inflammation, and/or irritation of innervated structures in the head1. These structures include:

  • Blood vessels1
  • Meninges1
  • Scalp1
  • Skull1

The supratentorial dura (most of the intracranial cavity) is innervated by the trigeminal nerve (CN V), while the dura of the posterior fossa is innervated mainly by CN X, but also by CN IX and the first three cervical nerves. The close anatomical relation between pain systems in the spinal trigeminal nucleus and the dorsal horn of the first few cervical nerve roots (see Figure 14.5C,D) may explain the common co-occurrence of headache and neck pain.

Classification

Primary headache

  • Migraine
  • Cluster headache
  • Cervicogenic Headache (Trigeminal autonomic cephalgias)
  • Tension-type headache

Secondary headache

  • Head trauma1
  • Intracranial hemorrhage1
  • Cerebral infarct1
  • Carotid or vertebral artery dissection1
  • Venous sinus thrombosis1
  • Reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome1
  • Post-ictal headache1
  • Hydrocephalus1
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (pseudotumor cerebri)1
  • Low CSF pressure Toxic or metabolic derangements1
  • Meningitis1
  • Epidural abscess1
  • Vasculitis1
  • Neoplasm1
  • Trigeminal and other cranial neuralgias1
  • Disorders of the eyes, ears, sinuses, teeth, joints, or scalp1
  • Psychiatric disorders1
