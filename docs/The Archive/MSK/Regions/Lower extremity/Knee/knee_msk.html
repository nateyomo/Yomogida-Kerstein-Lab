The knee region as a whole exists as an intermediate to link the hip and ankle/foot. This region consists of joints that primarily perform sagittal flexion and extension in order to control the distance between the body and the ground2.

Development

The patellofemoral joint (PFJ) does not even have a patella until ages 2-6 years. As a baby takes its first steps ~12mo, the lower extremity begins to adapt and develop with purpose2.

Joints

The joints of the knee work together to primarily create sagittal plane flexion-extension.

Flexion and extension are sagittal plane movements, thus the axis of rotation occurs in the frontal plane. This AOR passes through the femoral condyles.

Knee Joint Capsule

The knee joint capsule AKA the articular capsule refers to the fibrous sleeve that attaches the distal femur and proximal tibia. The inside of the knee joint capsule has a synovial lining2.

Overall, the shape of the capsule is a cylinder that indents posteriorly between the femoral condyles2. The knee joint capsule is not continuous, there is an anterior opening in the capsule where the patella passes through2.

The distal insertion of the knee joint capsules are distal to the tibiofemoral articulations2. The knee joint capsule attaches to the tibia just above the insertion of the popliteus tendon2.

The joint capsule lines the deep surface of the gastrocnemius heads and separates them from the condylar articular surfaces2. In this posterior region, the capsule is thickened and forms the posterior condylar plates2.

Synovium

The inner synovial membrane of the joint capsule secretes the synovial fluid2.

Synovial membrane

The synovial membrane is vascular and porous, thus allows diffusion of most molecules except for the largest ones2. The fluid that the synovial membrane allows through is called âsynovial fluidâ which is a filtrate of blood plasma2.

This is an important process for nutrient supply and waste removal from the avascular cartilage2.

Synovial fluid

The blood plasma is filtered through the synovial membrane and becomes âsynovial fluid.â The synovial fluid has a yellow color and is highly viscous2.

Synovial fluid builds up and forms a thin layer over the articular cartilage. Over time, the synovial fluid seeps into the micro-cavities and irregularities int he articular cartilage, which smooths the surface2.

During knee joint loading and movement, the synovial fluid is squeezed out of the cartilage and helps to maintain a fresh fluid layer on the cartilage surface2.

There are 2 primary functions of the synovial fluid:

  1. Nourish the articulation and associated cartilage2.
  2. Reduce friction between articular cartilage2.
  3. A tertiary role is to use phagocytotic cells to remove microbes and debris and minimize wear and tear2.

Plica

Plica refers to the fold or ridge of the synovial lining. Each fold differs in size and shape.

There are 4 major plica that can be present in adults:

  1. Supra-patellar plica (55%)2
  2. Infra-patellar plica (65%)2
  3. Medial patellar plica (25%)2
  4. Lateral patellar plica (1%)2

The Infrapatellar plica is an extension from the synovial lining and the inrapatellar fat pad2.

Sagittal flexion-extension is the primary movement of the knee.

Since the knee is a condylar joint and not a true hinge joint, it has secondary motions of axial rotation.
