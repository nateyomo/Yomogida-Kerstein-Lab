Reliability

Reliability is NOT the same as validity

1 Reliability significance

  1. One cannot be confident in the results of a scale with poor reliability1.
Example of Poor reliability
  • If a personâs score is 10 on 1 occasion and 15 on another (again assuming the person has not changed)1
  • If 1 rater gives a score of 7 and another a score of 12, we wouldnât be sure which, if either, score is correct1.
  • Similarly, if the items of a scale are not related to each other, we would not be sure just what it is that the scale is measuring (assuming that the scale is measuring a homogeneous construct)1.

It would be tantamount to measuring a piece of lumber with a rubber ruler, where each measurement yields a different length. Woe betide the carpenter who tries to cut the wood to fit into an opening. The second reason we want scales to be reliable is that an unreliable scale introduces measurement error, meaning that we would need a larger sample size in order to detect a difference between the groups; for a given difference, the lower the reliability, the larger the necessary sample size to declare the difference to be statistically significant.

3 Types of Reliability

3.1 Interrater Reliability

  • INTERârater reliability: Consistency of a measure assessed by multiple raters
  • Multiple PTs doing the test
  • Purpose: Determine the agreement between scores given independently by 2 practitioners1

The important point here is the necessity for the independence of the raters; 1 rater must be ignorant of the otherâs score. Otherwise, we would have a measure of the degree to which 1 person can accurately copy from another, which tells us much about the raters, but little about the subject.

Example of Poor Interrater reliability
  • If 1 rater gives a score of 7 and another a score of 12, we wouldnât be sure which, if either, score is correct1.
What to do with poor inter-rater reliability

How to improve inter-rater reliability: - The solution is usually more training of the raters1 - A common strategy is for raters to independently evaluate about 10 people who will not be part of the final sample, and to discuss why they disagreed on specific items; were the criteria unclear, ambiguous, poorly stated, or whatever? - This is repeated on a new group until satisfactory inter-rater reliability is achieved - If the study lasts more than a few months, it is usually a good idea to evaluate inter-rater reliability toward the middle to see if there has been any slippage in the level of agreement. - Problems exist with this method if thescale requires interviewing the subject1.

3.3 Test-Retest Reliability

Example

Test-Retest reliability refers to the similarity of scores when a study participant completes a test at Time 1, and then later at Time 21. If this test was reliable, we would expect the 2 scores to be similar, if not identical, on both occasions (assuming that the person has not changed in the interim)1.

When assessing test-retest reliability, the length of time between time 1 and 2 creates an issue since there is a fine balance the researchers must find with time since there is trade-off between 2 competing requirements1.

  • If the duration is too short, the participants will answer the questionnaire by memory in order to appear consistent1.
  • If the duration is too long, the personâs mood, anxiety, symptoms, or whatever we are measuring could change1.
  • The usual testretest interval is about 2 weeks, but this can be altered based on a number of factors1.
    • The Longer the scale the less time is required between measurements (the person is less likely to remember all of their answers)1.
    • Duration is also determined by how quickly the attribute being tested can change1.
What if my scale has low Test-Retest Reliability?
  • A self administered scale with poor test-retest reliability cannot be changed without altering psychometric validity1
  • A clinician-administered scale can be slightly changed by insuring that the instructions are clear and understood by the participant1

3.4 Internal Consistency

Internal consistency refers to the degree to which all of the items on a scale are correlated with one another1.

  • Internal consistency does not measure the patients or cliniciancs using the scale1.
  • Internal consistency measures how consistem the items of a scale are1.

3.4.1 Significance

  • Similarly, if the items of a scale are not related to each other, we would not be sure just what it is that the scale is measuring (assuming that the scale is measuring a homogeneous construct)1.

3.4.2 When to use

When to use internal consistency:

This is important for scales that measure 1 phenomenon:

Example

such as quality of life or depression

Internal consistency is not appropriate for:

  • Indices that measure things that are not expected to be correlated with each other1
Example

The Apgar scale consists of 5 items (heart rate, respiration, muscle tone, reflex response, and skin color)1. These may all occur together in healthy neonates, but in sick children a low score on one item does not necessarily mean there will be low scores on another item1.

5 Calculation

Reliability is the proportion of the total variance in scores that is due to differences among people1.

\[ Reliability = \frac{\sigma_s^2}{\sigma_s^2 + \sigma_e^2} = \frac{\sigma_s^2}{\sigma_Total^2} \]

  • \(\sigma_s^2\) refers to the variability between subjects1
  • \(\sigma_e^2\) refers to measurement error1
Note
  • Terms such as âprecision,ââagreement,â and ârepeatabilityâ focus solely on the fact that the error term should be as small as possible1.
  • These terms ignore the fact that reliability also depends on the variability among people1.
Subject variability implications
  • If there is no variability between participants ( \(\sigma_s^2\) ) then \(\sigma_s^2 = 0\) the reliability is 01.
    • The reliability of a scale reflects its ability to differentiate among people and if it cannot, then the reliability is 0 and the scale is useless1.
  • Another implication about \(\sigma_s^2\) is that reliability is not a fixed property, but rather dependent on the sample being studied.
    • Reliability is is very dependent on the sample in which it is determined1.
    • Example: Applying a depression scale to ER patients vs outpatient psychiatric patients
      • The ER Patients would consist of people who have very low depression to extremely high depression (suicidally depressed)1.
        • This sample has a very high patient variability ( \(\sigma_s^2\) ) and thus a higher reliability score1
      • The outpatient psychiatric patients would likely have moderate levels of depression (those with high depression would be administered to a hospital)1.
        • Thus there will be low patient variability (\(\sigma_s^2\)) and the reliability will be lower1.

7 Interpretation

How High Should Reliability be?

How high should reliability be?

  • Scales used in new, underdeveloped research areas should have a minimum reliability of 0.701.
  • Scales coming from mature areas of research, the minimum is 0.801.
  • and if the scale is to be used for clinical purposes the minimum is 0.90.

*Poor-moderate reliability are not good for clinical decision making since you are likely to get a different result everytime you test your patient, regardless of status
